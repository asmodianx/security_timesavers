About / Help

This tool walks a new analyst through source vetting, information credibility, sharing boundaries, and finished intelligence confidence. TLP shows who may receive the information; it is not a classification system. Admiralty separates source reliability from information credibility.

  • Use the wizard from top to bottom.
  • Review the recommendation and decision trace at each step.
  • If you override a recommendation, document the reason.
  • Print or export the output for case records or product appendices.

Step 0: Case Intake

Capture the case basics and the source or product context before scoring.

Checklist

  • Record the case identifier and analyst name.
  • Identify the source and collection method.
  • Capture the product type, scope, indicators, and key assumptions.
Why this matters

Analysts need context to judge whether a source is generally trustworthy, whether the specific information is credible, and how broadly a product can be shared.

Step 1: Source Reliability (Admiralty A–F)

Assess the source itself. Reliability reflects trust in the source, not whether this specific report is true.

Checklist

  • Review the source's track record and prior reporting.
  • Check source identity, provenance, or authenticity indicators.
  • Consider motive, bias, and proximity to the event.
Definitions and examples
  • A: Completely reliable
  • B: Usually reliable
  • C: Fairly reliable
  • D: Not usually reliable
  • E: Unreliable
  • F: Reliability cannot be judged

Why it matters: past performance is a strong indicator of future reporting reliability.

Why it matters: anonymous or poorly attributed reporting increases uncertainty.

Why it matters: domain expertise affects interpretation accuracy.

Why it matters: motive can reduce reliability even if details sound plausible.

Why it matters: firsthand collection is usually stronger than reposted or scraped content.

Why it matters: transparent sourcing supports independent review.

    Step 2: Information Credibility (Admiralty 1–6)

    Assess the content of the report. Credibility reflects whether the information itself is believable and supported.

    Checklist

    • Look for independent corroboration.
    • Check internal logic and consistency with known context.
    • Evaluate evidence quality and whether another analyst could reproduce the result.
    Definitions and examples
    • 1: Confirmed by other sources
    • 2: Probably true
    • 3: Possibly true
    • 4: Doubtful
    • 5: Improbable
    • 6: Truth cannot be judged

    Why it matters: multiple independent sources strengthen credibility.

    Why it matters: contradictions or logical gaps reduce believability.

    Why it matters: claims that fit known patterns are easier to accept, though novelty can still occur.

    Why it matters: primary evidence is more persuasive than secondhand claims.

    Why it matters: reproducible findings are more credible and teachable.

      Step 3: Sharing Marking (TLP 2.0)

      TLP defines sharing boundaries. It is not a formal classification system.

      Checklist

      • Identify the intended audience.
      • Estimate harm if the information is shared too widely.
      • Determine whether the information should remain inside one organization, one community, or be public.
      Definitions and examples
      • TLP:CLEAR — public release allowed.
      • TLP:GREEN — share within a community; not public posting.
      • TLP:AMBER — limited sharing on a need-to-know basis.
      • TLP:AMBER+STRICT — recipient organization only.
      • TLP:RED — highly restricted; do not share beyond named participants.

      Why it matters: audience scope is the core TLP decision factor.

      Why it matters: privacy, reputation, or operational harm should narrow dissemination.

      Why it matters: sharing permissions determine whether GREEN, AMBER, AMBER+STRICT, or RED fits best.

        Step 4: Product Confidence (Low / Moderate / High)

        Confidence reflects the quality, quantity, and consistency of the evidence. High confidence is not the same as certainty.

        Checklist

        • Count how many sources support the assessment.
        • Check whether different collection types agree.
        • Document gaps and assumptions explicitly.

        Why it matters: more supporting sources usually increase confidence if they are independent.

        Why it matters: variety reduces the risk of the same error repeating across sources.

        Why it matters: unresolved conflict lowers confidence.

        Why it matters: clear uncertainty statements improve analytic rigor.

        Why it matters: stale or partial data lowers analytic confidence.
